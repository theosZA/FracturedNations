British Isles

  • Shetland - (unsourced) Norse vassal of Iceland.
  • Alba - (source) King Donald II was the first king of the Picts to also be called King of Alba, the first step in the consolidation of the Scottish people.
  • Strathclyde - (unsourced) The remaining Scottish people opposed to the expansionism of Alba have consolidated themselves into the Kingdom of Strathclyde.
  • Jórvik - (source) The Viking Ivar the Boneless took this territory in 866. Subsequent wars against Anglo-Saxon and Norman England saw the boundaries shift but the Vikings were able to hold on to their new land. Like most land the Vikings captured, their culture has adapted to that of the local inhabitants, though they are one of the few nations to hang on to their Norse religion.
  • East Anglia - (unsourced) In 1066 the English army was defeated by the Norman William the Conquerer before facing a Norwegian army led by King Harald Hardrada. Although the Normans were victorious, they were greatly weakened by having had to fight an earlier battle against the English and were unable to destroy the Norwegian army. The Norwegians fell back to hold the area of East Anglia while the Normans held the rest of southern England.
  • Mercia - (source) The English civil war of 1444 say the primarily Anglo-Saxon English dukes overthrow their Norman king. The two strongest dukes were Wessex and Mercia and, once the war was won, the two were unable to agree who should now rule England leading to a stalemate in the divided kingdom.

  • London - (unsourced) The city of London fell into neither of the domains of the dukes of Wessex or Mercia and so was effectively independent once the Normans were defeated. The city was governed by a constitution which tried to maintain a balance of power between the merchants and nobles.

Formable nations

All nations (except reformed Holy Orders) when formed gain 25 prestige and claims on the rest of the nation's territory. Other than reforming Holy Orders, forming a nation will not change national ideas or government type.

The Papacy, the Holy Orders and the Crusader state, the Kingdom of Jerusalem, can't form nations (except the nations created by reforming the Holy Orders). Also the Empires and Cultural Unions (consolidated Holy Roman Empire, Byzantium, Latin Empire, Great Britain and Germany) are also excluded from forming any other nation. Subject nations are allowed to form nations that they qualify for, but colonial nations may not.

Normal nations - require 70%-80% of their territory to form

  • England - (ENG) Norman England only; excludes the east and north of England which is under the control of Danes and Norse. Requires English or Norman culture.
  • France - (FRA) Northern France only (i.e. Gallia). Requires French (i.e. Cosmopolitaine) or Burgundian culture.
  • Aquitaine - (source) Southern France. Requires Aquitaine or Occitain culture.
  • Spain - (SPA) Christian (Northern) Iberia only. Must be Christian.
  • Al-Andalus - (source) Muslim (Southern) Iberia only. Must be Muslim.
  • Italy - (ITA) Northern Italy only. Requires Umbrian or Lombard culture.
  • Naples - (NAP) Southern Italy only. Requires Sicilian culture and have Napoli as a core. The Catepanate of Italy may also form Naples if they choose Italian Ambitions in the appropriate event.
  • Austria - (HAB) Requires Austrian culture.
  • Poland - (POL) Core (northern) Poland only. Requires Polish or Schlesian culture.
  • Byzantium - (BYZ) Greece and Western Anatolia. Requires Greek culture. The Catepanate of Italy may only form Naples if they choose Greek Ambitions in the appropriate event.
  • Persia (PER)
