  • Vidin - (source) The second Bulgarian Empire collapsed under constant threat from the Byzantines, Hungarians and Mongols, resulting in the break-up of the Empire by the end of the 14th century. However the later weakness of the Bulgarian neighbours ensured that these successor states were never swallowed up.
  • Tarnovo - (source) Possibly the strongest of the successor states to the second Bulgarian Empire.
  • Dobruja - (source) Another successor state to the second Bulgarian Empire.
  • Albania - (ALB) vassal of Epirus

  • Corfu - (CEP) subject member of the Venetian League
  • Achaia - (ACH) vassal of Athens
  • Athens - (ATH)
  • Thessalonica - (source) Initially subject to the Latin Empire following the Fourth Crusade, Thessalonica became an independent Greek kingdom after the Empire's theocratic reform.
  • Latin Order - (source) The Latin Empire was established in the wake of the Fourth Crusade, but the Schism within the Church meant this Empire was doomed to fail. As the Empire lost control over its more distant Orthodox territories, Pope Innocent IV placed the Empire under the direct control of a Holy Order of the Church that became known as the Latin Order. If anything, this reform hastened the demise of this Crusader state.

North Africa

  • Ayyubid - (source) Rulers of Egypt from the 12th century until the 14th century when the Mamluk military attempted to overthrow the dynasty. Although the Ayyubids held on to much of their core territory, the civil war cost them control over the rest of Egypt and the Levant.
  • Mamluks - (MAM)
  • Nubia - (source) Christian Upper Egypt under the Muslim Ayyubids until their civil war with the Mamluks, giving them a chance to assert their independence.
  • Tripoli - (TRP)
  • Tunisia - (TUN)

  • Tlemcen - (source) Independent Ibadi kingdom in western Algiers.
  • Fex - (FEZ)
  • Ceuta - (unsourced) Small Sultanate established the Muslim Ceuti League as it struggled to increase it's influence in the western Mediterranean.
  • Anfa - (source) Under Morocco's Merinid dynasty, Anfa (Casablanca) increased in importance until it eventually established it's independence in the early 15th century. As the Merinids came under pressure from the Berbers of Fez, Tangiers came under the city's sphere of influence.
